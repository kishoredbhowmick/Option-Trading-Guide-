02 · Chart Reading

How to Read Price
Like a Professional

Before you touch options, you need to read the market's mood — trend, momentum, and key price levels.

Market trend types

Uptrend

Price makes higher highs and higher lows. Buyers are in control.

Downtrend

Price makes lower highs and lower lows. Sellers are in control.

Sideways

Price bounces between a ceiling and a floor with no clear direction. Best avoided by beginners.

Indicators, in plain English
EMA 21

A fast-moving average line. Shows short-term direction.

EMA 50

A medium-speed line. Confirms the trend is real, not noise.

EMA 200

The slow "big picture" line. Above it usually means bullish territory.

VWAP

The average price weighted by volume — shows the "fair price" today.

RSI

A 0–100 meter of momentum. Above 70 = overheated, below 30 = oversold.

Picking a timeframe
1m – 15m charts

Fast and noisy. Used for scalping — not a great place for a beginner to start.

1H – 4H charts

A good balance of signal versus noise. Best for beginner swing trades.

1D chart

Slow and reliable. Great for confirming the bigger overall trend.

EMA crossover — the fast line crossing above the slow line often signals a new uptrend
Support and resistance — price tends to bounce off these repeated levels
Candlestick basics
Each candle shows the open, high, low and close for one time period
Volume

Volume shows how many contracts traded. A big move on high volume is far more trustworthy than the same move on low volume.

03 · Core Concept

Understanding Options
Without Confusion

An option is a reservation. You pay a small fee to lock in a price — you're never forced to use it.

📈

Call Option — "I think price goes UP"

Buying a call means you profit if the price rises above your strike price before expiry.

📉

Put Option — "I think price goes DOWN"

Buying a put means you profit if the price falls below your strike price before expiry.

Four ways to trade options
Buy Call

Small, fixed cost. Profits if price rises. Best for beginners.

Buy Put

Small, fixed cost. Profits if price falls. Best for beginners.

Sell Call

Collects a fee upfront, but risk is large if price rises a lot. Advanced.

Sell Put

Collects a fee upfront, but risk is large if price falls a lot. Advanced.

A simple BTC example
Walkthrough

BTC is trading at $60,000. You believe it will rise, so you buy a call option with a strike price of $61,000, paying a premium of $50. If BTC rises to $63,000 before expiry, your option becomes valuable and you profit. If BTC stays below $61,000, your option expires worthless — and you only lose the $50 premium you paid. Nothing more.

Key terms glossary
TermMeaning
Strike PriceThe price level you're locking in.
PremiumThe small fee you pay to buy the option.
ExpiryThe date the option contract ends.
Intrinsic ValueThe "real" value if you exercised the option right now.
Time ValueExtra value that comes from time left until expiry.
ITMIn-the-money — the option already has real value.
ATMAt-the-money — the strike price is right at the current market price.
OTMOut-of-the-money — the option has no real value yet.
One formula worth remembering

Premium = Intrinsic Value + Time Value. As expiry gets closer, Time Value shrinks toward zero — that shrinking is the "time decay" you'll read about on Page 4.

Memory shortcut

📈 Call = Bullish   ·   📉 Put = Bearish

04 · Money Management

How Traders Make
and Lose Money

Winning trades don't make you profitable. Managing risk does.

Time Decay (Theta)

Options lose a little value every single day, even if price doesn't move. Time works against option buyers.

Volatility

How much and how fast price swings. Higher volatility means more expensive premiums.

Position Size

How much money you put into one trade. The number one cause of blown accounts.

Risk

The amount you could lose if the trade goes wrong. Define it before you enter.

Reward

The amount you could gain if the trade goes right.

Risk Management

The set of rules that keeps one bad trade from wiping out your account.

A first look at "the Greeks"
Delta

How much the option's price moves for every $1 move in BTC. Higher Delta behaves more like owning BTC directly.

Theta

How much value the option loses per day, purely from time passing.

Vega

How much the option's price changes when volatility itself changes.

Good trade vs bad trade

✓ Good trade

Clear trend, confirmed by EMA, VWAP and RSI together. Stop loss set before entry. Risking 1–2% of capital.

✗ Bad trade

Entered on a "feeling" during a sideways market. No stop loss. Risking 30% of capital on one trade.

A risk-to-reward ratio of 1:2 — you only need to be right 4 out of 10 times to profit
Capital protection example

With $10 total capital, risking 1–2% means risking just $0.10–$0.20 per trade — small enough to survive many losses while you learn.

Golden rule

Never risk more than 1–2% of your capital on a single trade.

05 · Execution

Step-by-Step First Trade
on Delta Exchange

A simple 12-step checklist to follow every single time — no skipping steps.

1
Open the BTC chart
Pull up the BTC chart on Delta Exchange.
2
Identify the trend
Is it trending up, down, or sideways?
3
Check the EMAs
Is price above or below EMA 21/50/200?
4
Check VWAP
Is price trading above or below VWAP?
5
Check RSI
Is momentum overheated, oversold, or neutral?
6
Choose call or put
Match your direction to the signals above.
7
Select a strike price
Usually pick one close to the current price (ATM).
8
Choose an expiry
Give the trade enough time to play out — not too short.
9
Set your stop loss
Decide your maximum loss before entering, not after.
10
Place the trade
Enter with a small size — 1–2% of capital.
11
Manage the trade
Let it play out — avoid emotional decisions mid-trade.
12
Exit and log it
Take the profit or accept the planned loss, then write it down.
Market order vs limit order

A market order fills instantly at whatever price is available. A limit order only fills at the price you set, or better — slower, but it gives you control and helps you avoid paying more than planned during a fast move.

One myth worth killing early

Profiting on a demo account does not guarantee profit with real money — live trades add slippage, fees, and emotions a demo never tests. Treat your first real month as still part of your education, not proof that you're ready to scale up.
